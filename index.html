Introduction¶

Chess is a two-player board game played by millions around the world. It is known for its complicated nature, which is why understanding it has been considered an interesting problem in computer science for decades. Since there is a finite number of combination of moves, one may think that a computer can just calculate through all combinations and solve the game.

This is unfortunately unfeasible: there are approximately $10^{120}$ possibles combinations of moves. For reference, there are approximatley $10^{80}$ atoms in the universe. By the time a computer calculated all the combinations, Earth would be no more.

Since brute force doesn't work, the obvious next approach is Machine Learning. In our project, instead of analyzing the entire chess game, we focus only on the opening, which is generally defined to be the first 20 moves of the game. We want to see whether or not one can use just the opening moves to predict the outcome of a chess game.

The dataset we use is generated from approximately 5 million chess games on lichess.org. The actual dataset was found on Kaggle, which can be found here:

  • https://www.kaggle.com/timhanewich/5-million-chess-game-results-november-2019

We have quite a few columns, so we will list and describe them here:

  1. Event - description of the match's type (i.e. rated vs casual match, in a tournament, etc).
  2. Result - whether the white won (1-0), black won (0-1), or a draw (1/2-1/2)
  3. WhiteElo and BlackElo - elo ratings of both players in the match
  4. WhiteRatingDifference - how white's elo differs from black's elo
  5. ECO - codes for chess opening used
  6. OpeningName - the opening used (if it was possible for lichess to determine)
  7. TimeControl - the starting time each player receives
  8. TimeIncrement - the amount of time gained per move made
  9. Termination - how the match ended (normal win, cheating/rules infraction, running out of time, abandoned/unterminated)
  10. Move1 to Move20 - indicates a move on the board; our dataset shows the first 20 moves (or less if the match ends earlier)

Data Cleaning¶

The next step is to clean our data. While cleaning data is usually one of the most tedious parts of the data analysis pipeline, it also saves us a lot of time and headaches when we want to perform actual analysis, such as visualizations and model training (both of which we will cover later on).

Keep in mind that there is no "one size fits all" step by step guide for data cleaning. But rather, there are certain guidelines or practices that one should follow. The goal of data cleaning is to end up with a tidy dataset that follows some standard (typically defined by your team) and is easy to work with when it comes to visualizing and analyzing.

Here are a couple of the guidelines that we follow when cleaning our hefty chess dataset (along with code-fragments that accomplish each of the cleaning tasks):

Removing unnecessary entries or rows¶

Datasets might include a lot of entries (or rows) that we do not need to look at. Specifically, our dataset contains two main types of matches: rated (competitive) and correspondence (casual). Since correspondence matches are less likely to be taken seriously than rated matches, we do not want to muddle our analysis with these "less serious" matches, where players are less likely to be playing at their best ability.

We also do not want to look at matches where players have cheated. For example, suppose a lot of players who cheat (and win) tend to use a certain opening. This could potentially bias our analysis in favor of "cheated" openings and lead us to believe that opening is most successful. However, in reality, that opening might not be very successful.

Handling missing data (or empty cells)¶

Often times, datasets will contain missing data or empty cells. As a result, this can cause unwanted errors, or even bad analysis, later down the road. So it is important to identify places where we have missing data and handle them accordingly (i.e. remove rows with missing data, attempt to impute data, use placeholder values, etc).

Fortunately in our case, most of our data is not missing. Though, there is one entry that is completely null/missing, but that is pretty easy to handle. Other than that, the only places where we have missing data are matches that end in less than 20 moves. Suppose a chess match ends in 13 moves. Then it makes sense that moves 14 through 20 will be "missing" since they cannot possibly exist.

To account for this, we will just remove matches that end in less than 20 moves because the empty moves pose problems later on for us when we train our models.

Converting columns into data types that are easier to work with analytically¶

Recall that the Result column gives us a bunch of strings. This is not super ideal if we want to look at win rates. So, we will convert the Result values into numerical values: 1 for a white win, 0 for a black win, and 0.5 for a draw.

Splitting up a single column into two or more columns.¶

Sometimes, a column might contain a bit too much information, so splitting up the column can make our dataset a bit more organized. For example, the OpeningName column contains two identifying parts of the opening: the base opening and its variant. In chess, players can make certain moves that identify with a "generalized" (or base) opening. Then their later moves might deviate a bit, and thus identify with a "variant" of their opening. This is particularly important since there can be many combinations of a base opening and variant openings, which can result in lower sample sizes for each combination. As a result, we will split the OpeningName column into its base and variant opening, and primarily analyze base openings.

For example, the opening "Italian Game: Giuoco Pianissimo" can be split into its base opening "Italian Game" and its variant "Giuoco Pianissimo".

Win rates based on Average Elo¶

We will group matches into 3 different average elo buckets (we will just refer "average elo" simply as "elo"):

  1. Beginner: elos less than or equal to 1600
  2. Intermediate: elos in between 1500 and 2000 (exclusive range)
  3. Expert: elos greater than or equal to 2000

We will then plot the win rates of the 5 most frequent openings for each elo bucket.

In [14]:
# https://matplotlib.org/gallery/api/barchart.html
# Labels the height/values of bars on plots
# Slightly modified to be pretty with decimals
def autolabel(ax, rects, xpos='center'):
    """
    Attach a text label above each bar in *rects*, displaying its height.

    *xpos* indicates which side to place the text w.r.t. the center of
    the bar. It can be one of the following {'center', 'right', 'left'}.
    """

    xpos = xpos.lower()  # normalize the case of the parameter
    ha = {'center': 'center', 'right': 'left', 'left': 'right'}
    offset = {'center': 0.5, 'right': 0.57, 'left': 0.43}  # x_txt = x + w*off

    for rect in rects:
        height = np.around(rect.get_height(), 4)
        ax.text(rect.get_x() + rect.get_width()*offset[xpos], 1.01*height,
                '{}'.format(height), ha=ha[xpos], va='bottom')

# Helper function to plot win rates from multiple dataframes
# n - How many of the largest openings to plot (defaults to 5)
def wr_subplots(dataframes, titles, axs, n=5):
    bars = []
    # Used for setting y-axis limits
    min_y = np.inf
    max_y = -np.inf

    for i in range(len(dataframes)):
        # Group dataframe by opening base
        df = dataframes[i]
        gb = df[['OpeningBase', 'Result']].groupby('OpeningBase')

        # Make new dataframe that uses opening base as row id
        # and has 2 new columns, Count and WinRate
        obd = gb.sum()
        obd['Count'] = gb.size()
        obd['WinRate'] = obd.Result / obd.Count
        # Dropping rows that have less than 1000 matches
        obd = obd[obd.Count >= 1000]

        # Taking 20 most frequent openings for this dataframe
        obd_frequent = obd.sort_values('Count', ascending=False)
        obd_frequent = obd_frequent[:n].reset_index()

        # Adding data to bar plot
        bar_container = axs[i].bar(obd_frequent.OpeningBase, obd_frequent.WinRate)
        axs[i].set_title('Win rates of %d most frequent openings\n%s' % (n, titles[i]))
        axs[i].set_xticklabels(obd_frequent.OpeningBase, rotation=45)

        # Adjust min/max y-values for plots
        # Will reflect these after we go through all dataframes
        min_y = np.minimum(min_y, obd.WinRate.min() - 0.01)
        max_y = np.maximum(max_y, obd.WinRate.max() + 0.01)

        bars.append(bar_container)

    for i in range(len(axs)):
        axs[i].set_ylim(min_y, max_y)

    return bars

We can also look at which openings are most successful based on elo difference. The main reason is that we want to see if certain openings are used depending on big of a skill gap there is between the players.

We will classify a "small" difference as elos that differ by no more than 45 points. Otherwise, elos that differ by at least 45 points would be considered a "large" difference.

Once again, we will also plot the 5 most frequent openings from each of these difference buckets.

On top of that, games with large elo differences have lower win rates for the common openings when compared with games with small elo differences. One likely explanation for this is that with a small elo difference, both players are closer to each other's skill level and thus more likely to either:

  • More accurately predict the other player's moves, or
  • Are better equipped (relative to their skill) to deal with certain situations or openings.

Our Model¶

The model we will use is a basic linear classifier with a twist. Note that since we are using only the chess moves to predict the result of the chess game, all our predicting variables are categorical variables. Most (if not all) models require predicting variables to be numerical, so we need to represent our moves differently.

The classical way to do this is one-hot encoding, which we talked about in class and you can read more about here. A problem with this is the high number of different chess moves (approximately 2500), causing the input data to be far too large for a machine to handle after the transformation. Another method to handle categorical variables is to use hand-made feature engineering. Though this performs better than one-hot encoding in practice, it takes a lot of time and testing to create sufficiently representative features.

Our idea is motivated from recent-ish developments in natural language processing (NLP) research. The idea is that words are very complicated in nature, so just using one number to represent them doesn't sufficiently encode their meanings. The improvement to one-hot encoding suggested is to use a vector of numbers to represent words, called word embeddings. These high dimensional vectors have empirically done well in representing the meanings of words. Traditionally, the vectors were trained using unsupervised learning methods, but they can also be treated as parameters of the model and optimized using gradient descent.

You can read more about word vectors here:

  1. Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space
  2. Global Vectors for Word Representation
  3. Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding

We apply this idea to chess moves: we create a vector embedding for each chess move. Then we concatenate the 20 vectors, put it through a linear layer, and then apply a softmax. Basically, it is just logistic regression extended to multiclass classification (note: it is multi-class since chess games can be drawn/tied).

In [19]:
class LinearClassifier(nn.Module):

    def __init__(self, input_dim, num_embeddings, output_dim=3, embedding_size = 50):
        super(LinearClassifier,self).__init__()
        self.Embedding = nn.Embedding(num_embeddings, embedding_size)
        self.Linear1 = nn.Linear(input_dim*embedding_size, output_dim)

    def forward(self, x):
        x = self.Embedding(x)
        x = torch.reshape(x,(x.shape[0],-1))
        return self.Linear1(x)

Training¶

Finally, we train our model. All of this complicated code just performs batch gradient descent.

train_losses = list()
valid_losses = list()

print("Beginning Training")

epochs = 100

for i in range(epochs):
    model.train()
    train_loss = 0

    #each for loop performs 1 gradient step
    for j, (inputs, targets) in enumerate(train_dl):
        inputs = inputs.to(device)
        targets = targets.to(device)

        optimizer.zero_grad()

        outputs = model(inputs)

        loss = loss_function(outputs, targets)
        loss.backward()

        train_loss += loss.item()

        optimizer.step()

    train_loss /= len(train_dl)

    model.eval()

    valid_loss = 0

    #this calculates the validation loss
    for j, (inputs, targets) in enumerate(valid_dl):

        inputs = inputs.to(device)
        targets = targets.to(device)
        outputs = model(inputs)

        loss = loss_function(outputs, targets)

        valid_loss += loss.item()

    valid_loss /= len(valid_dl)

    train_losses.append(train_loss)
    valid_losses.append(valid_loss)


    print(f"epoch:{i} Training | loss: {train_loss}   Validation | loss: {valid_loss}")

print("Training Complete")

Result #2¶

In natural language processing research, it has been shown that these vectors represent the meaning of words quite well. For example, the representation of King minus the representation of Man plus the representation of Woman, is actually very very close to the representation of Queen; that is King-Man+Woman=Queen. Towards Data Science has a couple good examples of visualizing word vectors, which you can find here:

  • Visualizing Word Vectors

We want to see whether we also have these meanings encoded in our embeddings. We test this by visualizing the embeddings, and seeing if there are any patterns. Since our embeddings have very high dimensions (which is hard to visualize), we first reduce the dimensionality using Principal Component Analysis (PCA), into 2 dimensions. Then we plot the different moves onto our classic xy-axis. We only plot a sample of the moves since plotting 2500 moves would just clutter everything.

Unfortunately, there doesn't seem to be any obvious patterns. There are a couple explanations for this. In NLP models, the word vectors are trained on more data and using deep learning (read: a lot of GPU time). Additionally, words naturally have more embedded meaning than a chess move.

Even though we couldn't interpret the embeddings, we showed that chess openings can predict the outcomes of games with accuracy significantly better than random.
